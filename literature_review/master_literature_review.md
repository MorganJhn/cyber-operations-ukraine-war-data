# Literature Review: Cyber Operations in the Russia-Ukraine War

## 1. Introduction & Scope

This literature review synthesizes 61 academic papers retrieved from Semantic Scholar across seven thematic domains relevant to the thesis research question: *What are the characteristics of cyber warfare based on the cyber domain of warfare in the Russia-Ukraine war (2022–2024)?* The review is organized around the core debates, dominant paradigms, and contested findings in each thematic area, with particular attention to gaps that the thesis's empirical findings can address.

The review draws on papers indexed in Zotero under the "PhD Thesis — Cyber Operations Russia-Ukraine" collection (64 items across 7 subcollections), retrieved via systematic keyword search in Semantic Scholar and supplemented by PDFs where available. Citations reference Zotero item keys in brackets [@ZoteroKey].

## 2. Theoretical Foundations: Neorealism and the Cyber Domain

### 2.1 The Offense-Defense Balance in Cyberspace

A foundational debate in cyber security studies concerns whether cyberspace is offense-dominant, defense-dominant, or whether the offense-defense distinction itself is meaningful. Slayton [@f3ae5383] argues that the concept of a cyber offense-defense balance is conceptually muddled, and that assessments depend heavily on what one counts as "costs" — her analysis of Stuxnet suggests the offense was significantly more expensive, challenging claims of inherent offense-dominance. Saltzman [@bbe19455] similarly contends that cyber posturing reveals an offense-defense balance that varies across contexts rather than favoring offense universally.

This directly challenges the neorealist assumption that cyberspace's structural features (low barriers to entry, attribution ambiguity) create a systemic offense-dominance. Blagden [@006aebff] shows that within offensive realism itself, the offense-defense balance affects conflict likelihood by shaping deterrence prospects — a finding that applies to cyber where attribution failures undermine deterrent signaling.

### 2.2 IR Theory and Cyber Security

Valeriano and Maness [@f0642d40] provide the most systematic application of IR theory to cyber security, arguing that cyber conflict follows patterns predictable by realist, liberal, and constructivist frameworks rather than being sui generis. Their work suggests that the Russia-Ukraine cyber conflict should exhibit patterns consistent with great power competition — restraint, signaling, and limited escalation — rather than unlimited cyber war.

Singh and Jash [@c5b1a3fd] apply offensive realism to US-China cyber competition, finding that technological nationalism and militarization of cyberspace intensify strategic competition. For the Russia-Ukraine case, this framework implies that cyber operations are not merely tactical tools but expressions of great power competition operationalized through the domain.

### 2.3 Key Theoretical Gap

The neorealist literature on cyberspace has largely developed independently of detailed empirical studies of the Russia-Ukraine conflict. The thesis can bridge this gap by testing whether neorealist predictions (offense-dominance, security dilemma dynamics, attribution-induced escalation risks) are borne out in the actual operational record.

## 3. Cyberwar Theory: What Makes Cyber Different?

### 3.1 Defining Cyber War

Hughes and Colarik [@b89dfbc1] construct a discourse hierarchy of cyber war definitions, demonstrating that definitions range from narrow (cyber operations causing physical destruction equivalent to armed attack) to broad (any politically motivated cyber operation). This definitional fragmentation complicates empirical research: whether an incident counts as "cyber warfare" depends on the definition adopted.

Wither [@2a8d89ba] situates cyber operations within the broader concept of hybrid warfare, emphasizing that cyber tools are rarely used in isolation but as components of multi-domain campaigns. The Russia-Ukraine war exemplifies this, with cyber operations preceding, accompanying, and following kinetic operations.

### 3.2 Deterrence and Strategic Stability

Nye [@bffc9f4c] provides the most cited treatment of cyber deterrence (237 citations), arguing that deterrence in cyberspace is possible but requires shifting from punishment to denial and entanglement strategies. His framework suggests that the limited cyber effects observed in Ukraine may reflect not Russian failure but a strategic choice to avoid escalation.

Taddeo [@2c3bf3f] complements this by arguing that deterrence alone is insufficient — international norms are necessary for stable cyberspace. Her argument gains empirical support from the Russia-Ukraine case, where norm-based restraint (avoiding attacks on certain targets) coexists with aggressive cyber operations.

### 3.3 The AI-Cyber Nexus

Johnson [@cecf5670] examines how AI-infused cyber capabilities affect escalation dynamics, arguing that the speed and autonomy of AI systems increase risks of inadvertent escalation. In the Ukraine context, this raises questions about whether AI-enhanced cyber operations (automated vulnerability scanning, AI-driven targeting) alter the character of cyber conflict.

### 3.4 Thesis Connection

The empirical finding that 47-51% of incidents are hacktivist DDoS (not sophisticated state operations) challenges cyberwar theory's focus on strategic attacks. The thesis can argue that theory must account for the "long tail" of low-sophistication operations that dominate the observable record.

## 4. The Russia-Ukraine Cyber Conflict: Empirical Landscape

### 4.1 The Limited Cyber Blitzkrieg Thesis

A central empirical debate concerns why Russian cyber operations did not produce the feared "cyber blitzkrieg" effect at the outset of the 2022 invasion. Willett [@da21f694] (70 citations) provides the most comprehensive early assessment, finding that Russian cyber operations were extensive but largely blunted by Ukrainian cyber resilience and Western assistance. He identifies a massive information battle for hearts and minds as the predominant cyber component.

Eichensehr [@a8043ea1] offers two competing explanations: either Russia's cyberattacks were thwarted by Ukrainian defenses, or Russia chose not to deploy them widely. Both explanations challenge pre-war assumptions about cyber power's decisiveness. The bitskrieg thesis is further explored by Brantly and Brantly [@79288571] (11 citations), who document persistent cyber operations at varying levels of success but find no strategic breakthrough.

Kostyuk and Gartzke [@69cb276] argue that cyber capabilities have limited coercive value in major war because the same factors that drive economic integration also incentivize restraint in cyber operations — a structural explanation consistent with neorealism.

### 4.2 Ukraine's Cyber Resilience

The effectiveness of Ukrainian cyber defense is a major finding across the literature. Miron and Thornton [@2066720c] analyze Russian Information Warfare doctrine, finding that Russian cyber-psychological operations aimed to complement kinetic effects but were constrained by Ukrainian resilience and Western cyber support.

Kostyuk and Brantly [@9369ce7] highlight the limits of interstate cooperation: despite extensive Western cyber assistance, Ukraine's cyber capabilities remain organizationally underdeveloped, and the limited fungibility of cyber tools complicates interoperability.

Wilde and Grossman [@fce66120] offer a unique perspective, arguing that Western tech companies' role in Ukraine's cyber defense represents a historically unprecedented shift — private industry has supplanted the state's traditional role in wartime cyber intelligence.

### 4.3 The Hacker Group Dimension

Biggio [@48585752] examines the legal status of hacker groups supporting Ukraine, arguing that the concept of combatancy is diminished in the cyber domain and that direct participation in hostilities must be adapted. This legal ambiguity directly maps onto the thesis's empirical finding of pervasive self-attribution and unclear actor boundaries.

Gray [@fbd9c47f] analyzes attribution, distinction, and self-defense challenges through the Russia-Ukraine case, finding that existing international law has "plot holes" in regulating cyberwarfare — a conclusion that resonates with the thesis's intercoder reliability findings (κ ≈ -0.007) showing that structured and full-text attribution produce divergent results.

### 4.4 Thesis Connection

These studies directly support the thesis's central empirical finding: the absence of cyber blitzkrieg and the predominance of low-intensity operations. The literature largely focuses on what cyber did NOT achieve, creating space for the thesis to characterize what cyber DID do — a detailed empirical mapping of operational characteristics.

## 5. Attribution: The Persistent Crisis

### 5.1 The Legal-Technical Attribution Divide

Tsagourias and Farrell [@013b9925] (28 citations) provide the definitive legal analysis of cyber attribution, examining both technical methodologies and international law standards. They find that responsibility gaps arise from mismatches between what technical attribution can establish (provenance, infrastructure) and what legal attribution requires (state control, effective authority). This divide maps precisely onto the thesis's intercoder reliability finding (κ ≈ -0.007) — structured database fields and full-text descriptions capture fundamentally different attribution signals.

Delerue [@65a15171] (60 citations) offers a comprehensive analysis of international law applicable to cyber operations, systematically examining attribution, lawfulness, and remedies. His conclusion that international law has limits in resolving issues related to cyber operations — particularly for state-sponsored non-state actors — directly supports the thesis's finding that zero cross-source overlap reflects complementarity, not failure.

### 5.2 False Flags and Ambiguity

Skopik and Pahi [@0da7a889] (56 citations) provide the most cited technical treatment of false flag attribution. Their analysis of how technical artifacts can be spoofed along the cyber kill chain directly informs the thesis's finding that 42.4% of incidents are self-attributed — if technical attribution is unreliable, who reports an incident matters enormously.

Brown and Fazal [@41b86afb] (18 citations) offer a crucial theoretical contribution: states neither confirm nor deny responsibility because non-denial denials serve strategic purposes — maintaining crisis stability while signaling capability. This finding explains why the thesis's attribution classification found such a high proportion of self-attribution: it reflects strategic choice, not merely technical limitation.

### 5.3 Attribution and Deterrence

Welburn, Grana, and Schwindt [@7cac06a] model cyber deterrence with imperfect attribution, finding that unverifiable signaling complicates deterrence but does not render it impossible. Nye [@bffc9f4c] similarly argues that attribution problems require adapting deterrence strategies rather than abandoning them.

Hedling and Ördén [@464a32e] extend attribution politics to disinformation, introducing the concept of the "uncertainty loop" where attribution decisions are charged with political risk. In the Ukraine context, this explains why states often defer formal attribution — the political costs of being wrong outweigh the benefits of being right.

### 5.4 Thesis Connection

The attribution literature provides robust theoretical support for the thesis's most striking finding: the intercoder reliability crisis (κ ≈ -0.007) between structured and full-text attribution. The thesis can argue that this is not a methodological failure but a substantive finding — it reveals a fundamental incommensurability between different attribution epistemologies (technical, legal, journalistic) that has been theoretically predicted but not empirically demonstrated at scale.

## 6. The Hacktivist Dimension: DDoS as Cyber Warfare

### 6.1 The Scale of Non-State Participation

A consistent finding across the literature is the outsized role of non-state actors in Ukraine's cyber conflict. Rodenhäuser [@a3121f04] examines the limits international humanitarian law imposes on civilian hackers, finding that while civilian hackers risk losing protection against attack when they directly participate in hostilities, their legal status remains ambiguous — a condition that enables the self-attribution pattern documented in the thesis.

Vu, Thomas, Collier et al. [@79af2629] provide the most direct empirical study of civilian cyber participation in the Russia-Ukraine conflict, finding that the narrative of "cyberwar fought by committed civilians and volunteer hacktivists" is misleading — there was clear loss of interest in defacements and DDoS after just a few weeks. This finding directly validates the thesis's granularity analysis showing that CISSM captures an initial surge of hacktivist activity that quickly attenuates.

### 6.2 Killnet and Escalatory Hacktivism

Derbyshire, Selck-Paulsson, Walt, and Burton [@ce32cf30] examine the strategic motivations behind "escalatory hacktivism," finding that states may tolerate, encourage, or leverage hacktivist groups as proxies. Their framework for interpreting hacktivists based on impact, ideology alignment, and state involvement provides theoretical language for the thesis's finding that hacktivist operations (47-51% of incidents) blur the line between spontaneous activism and state-adjacent operations.

Antwi et al. [@e489f09d] provide a detailed case study of KillNet's DDoS campaigns, finding that while DDoS rarely causes physical destruction, it degrades operational capabilities and drives defensive expenditures. The attribution challenges — decentralized structure, botnet-enabled scale, tool reuse, false-flag tactics — mirror the thesis's finding that hacktivist operations resist clear categorization.

### 6.3 Non-State Actors and International Law

Gabrielli [@04afcb9f] examines individual criminal responsibility of non-state actors in cyberspace under the ICC Statute, finding that the conditions for prosecution are stringent and often unmet. This legal gap helps explain why hacktivist operations persist despite their prevalence — there is no effective legal deterrent.

Schmoldt [@e0eec59] provides a comprehensive analysis of "patriotic hackers" under international law, offering a differentiated approach to classification. Her framework can help the thesis distinguish between types of hacktivist operations that the current dataset treats as uniform.

### 6.4 Thesis Connection

The thesis's finding that 47-51% of incidents are hacktivist DDoS dramatically exceeds the attention this category receives in the academic literature. Most studies focus on state APT operations or strategic cyber attacks. The thesis can argue that the hacktivist DDoS dominance is itself a structural characteristic of cyber warfare in this conflict — the low barriers to entry that neorealism identifies as a structural feature of cyberspace materialize as mass participation by non-state actors.

## 7. State-Sponsored Operations: APTs and Strategic Cyber

### 7.1 The APT Landscape

Katagiri [@2e515609] provides the most comprehensive empirical analysis of state-sponsored APT groups across China, Iran, Russia, and North Korea (the "big four"), finding that APT ties to authoritarian states are explained by strategic goals of supplementing military and economic power. His empirical support for the superiority of cyber offense over defense in these states directly supports the neorealist framework of the thesis.

### 7.2 Critical Infrastructure Targeting

Abraham, Houmb, and Erdődi [@fa40683a] provide an overview of APT targeting of energy infrastructure, documenting Industroyer2 (2022) as a case study. Their finding that APTs represent "low and slow" attacks that remain undetected until consequences manifest aligns with the thesis's two-layer observation model — the subsurface operations that CSIS/CFR capture are precisely these stealthy campaigns.

Falowo et al. [@74d8ad0c] provide a decadal longitudinal study of malware and DDoS attacks (2013-2023), finding a marked increase in DDoS in 2022 and steady rise in malware peaking in 2023. Their ARIMA model projecting continued patterns supports the thesis's argument that the Russia-Ukraine conflict marks a structural shift in cyber threat patterns, not a temporary spike.

### 7.3 Thesis Connection

The state-sponsored APT literature documents the subsurface layer that the thesis's two-layer model posits. The key gap: most APT studies focus on individual groups or campaigns, while the thesis provides a systematic, source-comparative perspective showing how different data collection methodologies capture different aspects of state-sponsored operations.

## 8. Synthesis: Toward a Neorealist Understanding of Cyberwarfare

### 8.1 What the Literature Establishes

1. **Cyber power is not decisive in major war** — consistent across Willett, Eichensehr, Brantly, Kostyuk/Gartzke. Cyber operations in Ukraine shaped the information environment and disrupted services but did not produce strategic military effects.
2. **Attribution is both a technical and a political problem** — consistent across Tsagourias/Farrell, Skopik/Pahi, Brown/Fazal. The attribution crisis documented in the thesis has theoretical grounding in this literature.
3. **Non-state actors are central, not peripheral** — consistent across Rodenhäuser, Vu et al., Derbyshire et al. The hacktivist DDoS dominance is not an anomaly but a structural feature.
4. **International law is under-equipped** — consistent across Delerue, Biggio, Gray, Gabrielli. Legal frameworks developed for kinetic conflict struggle with the cyber domain's characteristics.

### 8.2 What the Thesis Contributes

1. **Systematic empirical confirmation**: The 42.4% self-attribution rate empirically validates the "non-denial denial" theory (Brown/Fazal) at scale.
2. **The two-layer observation model**: Source comparison reveals that CSC/CFR and CISSM capture systematically different phenomena — a finding not addressed in the literature.
3. **The intercoder reliability paradox**: The κ ≈ -0.007 finding empirically demonstrates the incommensurability of attribution epistemologies that Tsagourias/Farrell and Delerue predict theoretically.
4. **DDoS as the dominant modality**: The 47-51% DDoS proportion challenges the literature's focus on sophisticated operations and supports neorealism's emphasis on low barriers to entry.

### 8.3 Gaps in the Literature Addressed by the Thesis

| Gap | Literature | Thesis Contribution |
|-----|-----------|-------------------|
| Multi-source comparison | Studies use single databases | Systematic CISSM vs CFR vs CSIS comparison |
| Attribution epistemology | Theoretical divergence | Empirical demonstration (κ ≈ -0.007) |
| Hacktivist quantification | Qualitative case studies | Quantitative characterization (47-51%) |
| Temporal granularity | Event-level or yearly | Monthly weighted analysis |
| Source methodology effects | Not examined | Pipeline audit traces source biases |
