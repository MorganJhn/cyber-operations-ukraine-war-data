# Gap Analysis: Thesis Empirical Findings vs. Existing Literature

## Finding 1: Zero Cross-Source Overlap (Complementarity, Not Triangulation)

**Literature says:** Studies typically use a single source database (CISSM alone, or CSIS alone) without systematic cross-source comparison. The attribution literature (Tsagourias/Farrell, Delerue) acknowledges epistemological pluralism but does not test it empirically.

**Gap:** No existing study systematically compares what CISSM, CFR, and CSIS each capture and miss. The thesis's finding that zero overlap exists between these sources — they capture complementary rather than shared events — is novel.

**Contribution:** The thesis can argue that multi-source integration is not a methodological convenience but an epistemological necessity. Different databases operationalize "cyber incident" differently, and the choice of database systematically shapes what the researcher observes.

---

## Finding 2: Intercoder Reliability κ ≈ -0.007

**Literature says:** The attribution divide is discussed theoretically (technical vs. legal attribution; Brown/Fazal's non-denial denial theory; Skopik/Pahi's false flag analysis). But the literature does not quantify the divergence between structured database attribution and full-text narrative attribution.

**Gap:** No study has systematically compared what structured database fields (actor, country, type) reveal versus what full-text analysis reveals about attribution. The thesis's pre-analysis intercoder reliability test is unprecedented.

**Contribution:** The κ ≈ -0.007 finding demonstrates empirically that structured and unstructured attribution are not converging on the same phenomenon. This is not noise — it's signal about the nature of attribution itself.

---

## Finding 3: Self-Attribution Dominance (42.4%)

**Literature says:** Brown/Fazal predict states will neither confirm nor deny, but their analysis focuses on state behavior, not database construction. The false flag literature (Skopik/Pahi) discusses how attribution can be manipulated.

**Gap:** No study quantifies the rate of self-attribution across a large incident corpus. The finding that 42.4% of incidents are self-attributed — reported by the affected party rather than an independent third party — has no direct precedent.

**Contribution:** The thesis can show that self-attribution is not a bug but a feature of cyber incident data collection. Sources that rely on media reporting (CISSM) disproportionately capture victim-reported incidents, while sources using intelligence/official sources (CSIS, CFR) capture state-attributed operations.

---

## Finding 4: Hacktivist DDoS Dominance (47-51%)

**Literature says:** Academic attention focuses disproportionately on sophisticated state operations (APTs, destructive malware, espionage). The hacktivism literature (Derbyshire, Rodenhäuser, Vu et al.) is newer and largely qualitative.

**Gap:** No study has quantified hacktivist DDoS as the majority of cyber operations in armed conflict. The thesis's finding challenges the implicit assumption that cyber warfare is primarily a state-versus-state domain.

**Contribution:** The thesis can argue that the structural characteristics of cyberspace (low barriers to entry, attribution ambiguity) predicted by neorealism materialize empirically as mass participation by low-sophistication actors. DDoS dominance is a structural feature, not a temporary anomaly.

---

## Finding 5: Two-Layer Observation Model (Surface vs. Subsurface)

**Literature says:** The Ukraine literature (Willett, Eichensehr, Brantly) focuses on particular campaigns or incident categories. No study compares how different data collection methodologies capture different "layers" of cyber activity.

**Gap:** The thesis's finding that CISSM captures a surface layer (mass events, DDoS, defacement) while CSIS/CFR capture a subsurface layer (strategic operations, espionage, destruction) has no precedent in the literature.

**Contribution:** The two-layer model provides a methodological framework for future multi-source cyber conflict research. It explains contradictory findings in the literature — studies using CISSM vs. CSIS will reach different conclusions about the nature of cyber warfare because they are observing different phenomena.
